About

I’m a PhD student in the Knowles Group at the University of Cambridge,working on designing proteins that can intervene in amyloid aggregation, the process that drives neurodegenerative diseases like Parkinson's and Alzheimer's.

My work combines computational protein design with quantitative biophysics: I use deep-learning tools to create de novo binders against intrinsically disordered targets, then use microfluidics, chemical kinetics, and biophysical assays to dissect which microscopic step of the aggregation cascade each molecule intercepts. The goal is a rational framework for designing proteins that reshape aggregation pathways rather than just slowing them down.

Research

My current research involves designing de novo proteins which target alpha synuclein, the key driver in Parkinson's disease.

De novo protein design

Using deep-learning methods (structure prediction, diffusion-based design, and sequence generation), we generated compact single-chain binders against intrinsically disordered targets that lack a stable epitope for conventional discovery.

Amyloid aggregation kinetics

We resolved the individual microscopic steps which the designed proteins act on by applying ThT aggregation assays.

Targeting neurodegeneration

Focusing on α-synuclein and the toxic oligomeric species linked to Parkinson’s disease, we aim to selectively capture on-pathway intermediates at substoichiometric ratios.

Publications

Preprint · 2026

Potent substoichiometric inhibition of α-synuclein aggregation by de novo oligomer-binding proteins

Y. Zhang, H. L. Han, L. Ortigosa-Pascual, U. Z. Miles, F. Snow, D. Tu, G. Meisl, T. J. Nott, H. Laman, A. C. McShan, D. D. Sahtoe, T. P. J. Knowles

bioRxiv (2026) · Biophysics · doi:10.64898/2026.07.13.738012

Abstract

The non-amyloid β component (NAC) domain of alpha synuclein (Syn) drives Syn aggregation in Parkinson disease, yet as an intrinsically disordered segment it lacks a stable epitope for conventional ligand discovery. Using deep learning-based protein design, we generated compact single-chain binders that take advantage of the propensity of the NAC domain to adopt an extended β-strand conformation, which they engage and stabilise. From 21 expressed designs, 3 engaged the target both in vitro and within live cells. A single round of partial diffusion improved their affinity, maturing the strongest binder to a dissociation constant of 1.94 nM with no detectable cross-reactivity to tau, amylin or amyloid β. Solution NMR spectroscopy confirmed Syn peptide association with all three binders and showed that peptide binding induces conformational changes consistent with the intended design architecture. Each binder engaged a distinct point on the aggregation pathway. Kinetic analysis combining seeded aggregation with the measured affinities for monomer, oligomer and fibril resolved the specific microscopic step that each binder inhibits. Notably, the most potent binder acted by selectively capturing on-pathway oligomers, the species most closely linked to toxicity, and suppressed fibril formation at substoichiometric ratios without engaging the bulk monomer. Together, these findings establish de novo-designed Syn binders that selectively target distinct aggregation intermediates to mechanistically reshape Syn assembly, providing a framework for the rational design of aggregation-modifying proteins.
